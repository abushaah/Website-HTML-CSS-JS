Criminal law is law that identifies crimes and prescribes punishment

Crime

  • Crime is an act or omission of an act that is prohibited and punishable by a federal statute.

  • The purpose of criminal laws is to protect people and property, maintain order, and preserve standards of public decency.

  • Four conditions must exist for an act or omission to be considered a crime:
    1. The act is considered wrong by society.
    2. The act causes harm to society in general or to those who need protection.
    3. The harm must be serious.
    4. The remedy must be handled by the criminal justice system.

  • Elements of a crime

    1. Actus reus

      is the “the guilty act” itself, such as the voluntary action, omission, or state of being that is forbidden by the criminal code.

    2. Mens rea

      is the deliberate intention to commit a wrongful act, with reckless disregard for the consequences.

      Mens rea can be shown through Definition Example
      Knowledge The awareness of certain facts that can be used to establish mens rea. Using a contract/document that you know is forged.
      Criminal negligance Wanton or reckless disregard for the lives and safety of others, sometimes causing serious injury or death. Leaving a loaded firearm where a child could access it.
      Recklessness Consciously taking an unjustifiable risk that a reasonable person would not take. Not wearing glasses while driving when required to.
      Wilful blindness A deliberate closing of one's mind to the possible consequences of one's actions. Buying a laptop with UOGuelph engraved on it.

    3. Intent

      is the state of mind and willingness to commit a crime.
      1. General intent is without ulterior motive or purpose.
      2. Specific intent is for the sake of accomplishing another goal.

    4. Motive

      is the reason a person commits a crime (Jealousy? Greed? Revenge?).

    5. Strict liability offences

      are offences that do not require mens rea but to which the accused can offer the defence of due diligence.

    6. Due diligence

      is the defence that the accused took every reasonable precaution to avoid committing the offence.

    7. Absolute liability offences

      are offences that do not require mens rea and to which the accused can offer no defence.

  • The two elements that must be present in most cases for an action to be considered a criminal offence under the Canadian law are actus reus, the act itself, and mens rea, the intention to commit the act.

Involvement in a Crime

  • Perpetrator: the person who actually commits the crime. If 2 or more, they are referred to as co-perpetrators.

  • Parties to an offence: are people not directly involved in the crime but considered partly responsible for it.
    1. Aiding: helping the perpetrator commit the crime.
    2. Abetting: encouraging the perpetrator to commit the crime.
    3. Counselling: advising, recommending, or persuading another person to commit a crime.
    4. Accessory after the fact: someone who knowingly assists a perpetrator in escaping from the police.
    5. Party to a common intention: the shared responsibility among criminals for any additional offences that are committed in the course of the crime they originally intend to commit.

  • Incomplete crimes
    1. Attempt: the intention to commit a crime, even if the attempt is unsuccessful or not completed. The Crown or Prosecution has to show the accused had the necessary intent and took steps towards committing the crime.
    2. Conspiracy: an agreement between two or more people to carry out an illegal act, even if that act does not actually occur.

Criminal sentencing: types of offences

  • Summary offences are minor or less serious offences (careless driving). They are dealt with quickly and held in a Provincial court before a judge. [Sentence = $2,000 or 6 months in jail].

  • Indictable offences are more serious offences (murder, robbery). The trial is held in Provincial or Superior court, depending on severity, before a judge or a judge and jury. [Sentence = 2 years to life in prison].

  • Hybrid offences are a summary or inductive offence, depending on circumstances (theft under $5,000, dangerous operation of a vehicle). They are treated as indictable until the court decides it is a summary offence by looking at the type of offences and criminal past of the accused.
