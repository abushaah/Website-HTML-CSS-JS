Civil law, also known as Private law, deals with disputes between people and/or companies

Tort Law

  • The tort is harm caused to a person or property that can create a civil action. It can be classified as either intentional torts or unintentional torts.

    1. Negligence and unintentional tort
      • Unintentional tort are injuries caused by an accident or action that was NOT intended to cause harm.
      • Negligence is careless actions that harm someone, however, the harm was foreseeable.
      • There are three factors that must be present for a case to be considered negligence and unintentional tort:
        1. Duty of Care - the defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care.
        2. Standard of Care - determining how much care the defendant owes.
        3. Causation - the plaintiff must prove that the actions of the defendant actually caused their injuries.

    2. Intentional tort
      • Intentional torts are actions purposely done to cause injury to others.
      • The same event can result in two distinct consequences:
      • Criminal law - bringing the accused to justice.
      • Civil law - to compensate victims.

Family law

  1. Requirements for a Marriage
    • Mental Capacity - the ability to understand the marriage contract and the responsibilities and duties involved in marriage.
    • Freedom of Consent - must be entering the marriage willingly.
    • Age of Consent - in Canada, you must be at least 18 years old to get married, those less than 18 need consent from their parents.
    • Absence of a Prohibited Relationship - people who intend to marry cannot be too closely related to each other by blood.
    • Termination of Prior Marriages - a person can only legally be married to one spouse at a time (however, there are changing marriage trends).
    • Sexual Capacity - consummation of the marriage.

  2. Formal Requirements for Marriage
    1. Marriage License - a legal document authorizing two people to marry.
    2. Marriage Ceremony
      • Must be witnessed by at least two people 18 years of age or older.
      • Must be conducted by someone who is authorized by law to do so (a member of a religious organization, a judge, or a Justice of the Peace).

      • Each party must declare that they are not aware of any impediment to the marriage.
      • Each party must take the other as their “lawful wedded” partner.
      • The person performing the ceremony must pronounce the couple.

  3. Ending a marriage
    • Divorce - the legal termination of a marriage.
    • The person seeking a divorce is the applicant, while the other person is the respondent.
    • An Application for Divorce provides reasons for the divorce and requests for arrangements regarding support payments and child custody.
    • “Marriage Breakdown” must be proven. For example, if spouses “live apart” for one year, one spouse commits adultery, or one spouse has been physically or mentally abusive.
