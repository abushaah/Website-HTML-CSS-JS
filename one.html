Defences of the court

Defence is either the denial of having committed a crime or a justification for the act. If the accused acknowledges they committed the crime but have a good reason, they must offer proof.

  • Defence

    1. Mental States: a mental disorder where the accused cannot be held responsible (no mens rea).
        The defence counsel must show either:
      • the accused did not know what they were doing, or;
      • the accused did not know the act was wrong.
        • If accepted, the accused may be set free or sent to a psychiatric hospital.
    2. Automatism: the accused acts without being aware of what they are doing (no actus reus).
    3. Intoxication: the condition of being overpowered by drugs or alcohol to the point of losing self-control. This defence only reduces the charge from a specific intent crime to a general intent crime.

  • Justifications

    1. Self-Defence: defence from an unprovoked attack. The force has to be reasonable and can only be enough to fend off the attack.
    2. Battered Woman Syndrome: the effects of prolonged spousal abuse.
    3. Defence of a Dwelling: a person can defend their dwelling from any unlawful entry and remove a trespasser if they have entered. The force used must be reasonable.
    4. Necessity: the accused had no other choice or opportunity for alternative action but to commit the criminal act.
    5. Compulsion or Duress: the accused was forced to commit a criminal act against their will by threat of violence. This defence cannot be used as a defence for violent crimes (murder, sexual assault, robbery, or assault with a weapon).
    6. Provocation: any act or insult that causes someone to lose control.

  • Other defences

    1. Mistake of Law: the ignorance of the law where the accused relied on incorrect legal advice from a law enforcement official.
    2. Mistake of Fact: the accused made an honest mistake that led to the breaking of the law (no mens rea).
    3. Double Jeopardy: an accused person cannot be tried twice for the same offence, even if new evidence is discovered after the acquittal.
    4. Alibi: the accused claims that they were somewhere else when the offence was committed.
    5. Entrapment: police conduct that illegally induces criminal behaviour.
